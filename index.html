Abstract

Predicting a material's behavior directly from its atomic structure is a longstanding goal of the physical sciences and a prerequisite for accelerating the discovery of new functional materials. The density of states (DOS), which describes the distribution of quantum states across energy, is central to materials properties: the electronic DOS (eDOS) governs electronic and optical behavior, while the phonon DOS (phDOS) determines vibrational and thermodynamic responses. These spectra are conventionally obtained from density functional theory (DFT), which is accurate but costly, motivating machine-learning surrogates that map a crystal structure directly to its full spectrum. Yet existing crystal-to-spectrum models decode the spectrum from a single pooled embedding, reconcile band gaps only post hoc, and rely on pointwise regression that blurs band edges and violates total-state conservation. Here we introduce DeepDOSReasoner, a physics-grounded framework that produces energy-resolved predictions through atom-to-energy source attention and refines the normalized DOS under a continuity-equation transport that enforces non-negativity and total-state conservation by construction. DeepDOSReasoner advances the state of the art in learning-based DOS prediction, outperforming existing crystal-to-spectrum models across all major eDOS evaluation metrics, recovering element-resolved contributions without explicit supervision, identifying rare near-Fermi valence-band gaps with high fidelity, and transferring with minimal modification to phDOS prediction. On prospective materials-discovery tasks validated by new DFT calculations, DeepDOSReasoner consistently outperforms existing approaches. Three case studies further demonstrate transfer across functional-materials classes: substituent-induced variants of two parent compounds, photovoltaic-relevant I2–II–IV–X4 chalcogenides, and a CuPtFeCoNi high-entropy alloy (eDOS center within 0.2 eV of DFT). DeepDOSReasoner's superior performance stems from the seamless integration of data-driven learning with first-principles physical reasoning: its neural decoder learns rich structure-to-spectrum representations from data, while its constraint-preserving reasoning stage explicitly enforces fundamental conservation laws and physical consistency. Ablation studies demonstrate that neither component alone is sufficient and that the strongest results emerge from their tight coupling. Moreover, DeepDOSReasoner remains highly sample-efficient, matching strong baselines using only 25% of the training data, which paves the way for its application to spectrum-prediction tasks where limited training data challenges prior methods. More broadly, DeepDOSReasoner provides a general blueprint for integrating scientific knowledge and machine learning through first-principles physical reasoning, opening a path toward high-throughput screening of electronic and vibrational properties and the autonomous discovery of functional materials.

How it works

DeepDOSReasoner couples a structure-aware neural decoder with a physics-grounded reasoning stage. The shape of the spectrum is learned from data; its total magnitude is fixed by chemistry, and conservation is guaranteed by the integrator itself rather than encouraged by a soft penalty.

Stage I

Structure-aware spectrum decoding

An edge-aware crystal-graph Transformer encodes atoms and interatomic features. A decoder then produces the spectrum bin-by-bin through atom-to-energy source attention: each energy bin selectively aggregates atom-level information, grounding every spectral coordinate in the atoms that physically contribute at that energy — and recovering element-resolved contributions with no explicit supervision.

Stage II

Constraint-preserving physical reasoning

The normalized DOS is treated as a probability density and refined by a learned continuity-equation transport with no-flux boundaries. A CDF-based upwind scheme moves spectral mass along the energy axis while keeping the spectrum non-negative and conserving total states by construction. Mass can only be redistributed — never created or destroyed — so the output is a valid density at every step.
